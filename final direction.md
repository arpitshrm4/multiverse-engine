Objective

The audience is:

International Design Manager
Lead Product Designer

Head of Design

1. Simplify the Language

Current writing is still too technical.

Many headings sound like architecture documentation instead of product storytelling.

The rule is simple:

If a VP of Product cannot understand the heading in three seconds, rewrite it.

Avoid terminology that feels AI-generated or overly technical.

7. Make Reflection Personal

Current reflection still sounds generic.

Instead explain

How this project changed the way I think about design leadership.

Example direction

I entered this project expecting to solve interface problems.

Instead, I learned that the hardest design challenges were organizational—aligning policy, engineering, domain experts, and user needs into one coherent product strategy.

Reflection should sound like a Design Manager.

Not a textbook.

8. Validate Every Claim

Separate

Observed Problem

↓

Design Decision

↓

Verified Outcome

Never mix them.

Example

Observed Problem

The legacy platform relied on fragmented browsing and inconsistent metadata.

Decision

We redesigned discovery around search.

Verified Outcome

The platform now supports discovery across 14.3M+ manuscripts from 60+ participating institutions.

Avoid unsupported claims like

Reduced search time by X

Improved lookup speed

Eliminated classification silos

Unless verified.

14. Final Quality Checklist

Review the complete case study and remove anything that feels AI-generated.

Ask these questions:

Does this sound like an experienced Design Manager?
Is every claim defensible?
Can a VP of Product understand every heading?
Is any sentence overly dramatic?
Is every technical term necessary?
Can any paragraph be reduced by 30%?
Is the user benefit always clearer than the implementation?
Does every Turning Point feel like a memorable story?

If the answer is "no," revise it.

Success Criteria

By the end of the case study, an international hiring manager should think:

This person can lead complex digital transformation initiatives.
They make thoughtful product decisions under ambiguity.
They understand systems, not just screens.
They balance business, technology, and user needs.
They communicate clearly and credibly.
They know what to build—and what not to build.
I would trust them to lead a product design team.
Final Creative Principle

Don't explain the product. Explain the decisions that changed the product.

Every page should answer one question:

"Why was this decision made, and what changed because of it?"

If every chapter follows that principle, your portfolio will feel less like a UX case study and more like the story of a design leader guiding a national-scale product transformation. That distinction is exactly what will resonate with international Design Manager and Head of Design hiring managers.
